Book miniatures: Artaud, Héliogabale ou l’Anarchiste couronné

This is a book, perhaps a biography of sorts, about the Roman emperor Elagabalus from the Severan dynasty, easily one of the most de- and contested figures in imperial history. Born in Syria, Elagabulus was raised as the high priest of the local sun god Elagabal and ascended to the throne at the age of 14, succeeding the praetorian prefect Macrinus who had killed his relative (and alleged father) Caracalla. Elagabalus is more than just a spoilt teenager; his depravities, mostly sexual in nature, are legion. In the interest of keeping this post family-friendly (for whatever reason), I omit the details. Having offended the Roman sense of decency and morality more than any emperor before or since, he is dead at 18. Contemporary historians agree that his scandalous reputation is most likely to some extent the result of fabrications by later historians, who couldn’t resist adding yet another lurid story to the record of the degenerate pervert from the East. But this isn’t really Artaud’s concern.

Artaud is best described as a 20th century surrealist-leaning weird French guy who produced texts of various, mostly undefined, genres while working as an actor and director on the side. He specialized in violence, shock and transgression. In my internal mind map of French writers, I group him together with people like Bataille and Genet. Artaud sees Elagabalus as a precursor to anarchism, a destroyer of bourgeois morality avant la lettre, a revolutionary force disrupting the ancient world:

L’anarchiste dit: Ni Dieu ni maître, moi tout seul. Héliogabale, une fois sur le trône, n’accepte aucune loi; et il est le maître. Sa propre loi personnelle sera donc la loi de tous. Tout tyran n’est au fond qu’un anarchiste qui a pris la couronne et qui met le monde à son pas.

The anarchist says: Neither God nor master, me alone. Once on the throne, Elagabalus doesn’t accept any law; and he is the master. His own personal law will thus become the law for everyone. Every tyrant is essentially nothing other than an anarchist who has taken up the crown and forces the world to obey.

Anarchism here describes not merely a form of political organization but also, perhaps primarily, a way of thinking, of organizing the world mentally:

Avoir le sens de l’unité profonde des choses, c’est avoir le sens de l’anarchie, – et de l’effort à faire pour réduire les choses en les ramenant à l’unité. Qui a le sens de l’unité a le sens de la multiplicité des choses, de cette poussière d’aspects par lesquels il faut passer pour les réduire et les détruire.

Having a sense of the profound unity of things means having a sense of anarchy – and of the effort it takes to reduce things by bringing them back to unity. Those who have a sense of unity have a sense of the multiplicity of things, of this dust of aspects through which one must pass to reduce and destroy them.

To be an anarchist means to understand the tension and the dynamics between unity and multiplicity, between the one and the many. The subversive power of Elagabalus (who, by today’s standards, would be considered trans) lies in the way in which he both asserts and transcends boundaries: between male and female, order and chaos, rhythm and discordance, grandeur and childishness, generosity and cruelty. All these principles, Artaud asserts, ne valent que pour l’esprit qui pense, et quand il pense; mais hors de l’esprit qui pense, un principe se réduit à rien (are only valid for the thinking mind, and for when it thinks; but outside of the thinking mind, a principle reduces to nothing).

If you sense a whiff of poststructuralism here, you’re absolutely right. Both Deleuze and Derrida wrote extensively about Artaud. We’re in a world where all dichotomies are false and all binaries must be deconstructed. I’ve never fully grasped the allure of it all, and ultimately found Héliogabale ou l’Anarchiste couronné surprisingly boring. Yes, of course all dichotomies are false. Artaud does his best to shock the reader (good luck with that in the 21st century) and convey how incredibly transgressive his work is, but from my very humble perspective, he ultimately comes across as an obnoxious teenager who has discovered atheism for the first time and won’t shut up about it. I'm realizing that at this point I’m just trying to articulate a general discontent I’ve always had with Deleuze (and, perhaps even more so, with certain people I used to know who liked Deleuze), and I’ve been meaning to re-read Mille Plateaux for ages now anyway, so I’ll stop be here and be back with a post about that, probably in a few months.
