Book miniatures: Chadwick, The Decipherment of Linear B

Linear B, for the non-nerds among us, is an ancient script of Mycenaean Greek predominantly found on clay tablets in Minoan Crete. It was first deciphered in the 1950s, a discovery almost as groundbreaking as – albeit admittedly less famous than – the Rosetta Stone. Part of the challenge was to recognize that Linear B was written in Greek at all, not necessarily expected for a script dating back to the Late Bronze Age, when the ethnic and linguistic landscape of the Eastern Mediterranean still looked very different. Chadwick’s book provides a brief overview of the discovery of the script, its decipherment, and the insights it offers into life in the Mycenaean world prior to the Late Bronze Age collapse.

I recently noticed that whenever I’m knee-deep in work and don’t have much headspace for anything else, I tend to gravitate towards extremely matter-of-fact non-fiction books that Normal People would consider unbearably dry. I find the existence of facts deeply soothing. Linear B is Greek. It is a syllable script. Mycenaean Greek was an Arcadian dialect. It has a case ending that hasn’t survived even in Homer. What better way to spend a quiet evening at home. Reading a novel, watching a movie, having emotions – all of that would be too much.
