Book miniatures: Sei, The Pillow Book

In Western terms, The Pillow Book is a blend of Montaigne and Oscar Wilde. Like the Essays, it is a collection of personal reflections with no clear guiding theme or structure; like Wilde’s work, it is entirely aesthetic in perspective. Sei Shōnagon was a gentlewoman at the court of the Japanese emperor in the tenth to eleventh century CE. Her life was extremely secluded; nothing major happened to her, or if it did, she does not write about it. She is far more interested in some witty remark someone made at court, what color to wear in fall, or how enchanting it is to take a walk in the moonlight. She delights in making lists, which range from the trivial to the whimsical to the insightful. Here are some abridged samples:

Things that are near yet far – Relationships between siblings or relatives who don’t like each other. The first day of the new year, seen from the last day of the old.

Things that are far yet near – Paradise. Relations between men and women.

Things that create the appearance of deep emotion – The sound of your voice when you’re constantly blowing your runny nose as you talk. Plucking your eyebrows.

Things that just keep passing by – A boat with its sail up. People’s age. Spring. Summer. Autumn. Winter.

Things that are repulsive – A very ordinary woman looking after lots of children.

Sei (this is her last name) was apparently not what one would usually call a nice person. She has nothing but disdain for the lower classes; like Montaigne, she is self-centered to a fault; like Wilde, the notion of moral values seems foreign to her. Even personal suffering and religion are merely a spectacle for her:

It’s engaging to witness some very attractive young lady of eighteen or nineteen, with a beautiful sleek head of hair hanging the length of her back, wonderfully thick at the ends, a lovely plump figure and very pale face, who is suffering from a terrible toothache. She sits there, hand pressed to her bright red cheek, quite unaware that her side-locks are all sodden from weeping.

Ideally, a priest should have a great reputation and be in constant demand hither and yon, and he should be accompanied by numerous assistants – handsome young boys with beautiful hair, or larger lads with surprisingly lovely hair despite their sprouting beards, or else powerfully built boys whose hair is almost disgustingly thick.

I find this utterly refreshing. As an upper-middle-class white woman in her early thirties living in the American Northeast, I am constantly surrounded by people who have excellent moral values and exercise them relentlessly all the time. I’m all for it, and I’m glad that society has advanced to this point, but to quote Wilde, the thing has become an absolute nuisance. To reiterate, I fully endorse the whole gamut of equality and social justice and everything, and I do not ultimately think that virtue signalling should be considered a genuine societal problem when we also have a nascent fascism situation on our hands, but when conservatives complain about excessive moral policing of thought and speech, on some level I do understand. One of the things that I love about reading books that are from a very different era is not having to constantly wonder whether what I’m reading is appropriate and in line with my values. By modern standards, Sei would be bigoted and mean, but modern standards don’t apply to her, so I don’t have to get upset about her being bigoted and mean and can just find it hilarious. To end on a final quote:

Everything that cries at night is wonderful. With the exception, of course, of babies.
