Book miniatures: Gleick, Chaos

This is a book about chaos theory. I won’t try to explain what chaos theory is, partly because it’s my second post today and I’m tired, partly because I’m entertaining the thought of starting an official blog about linguistics-related things on my official website, and one of the things that would be fun to write about is whether chaos theory is applicable to language. So I’ll set this aside for now.

I’ve read this book twice now, and each time it has reminded me of why I’m in love with science. Chaos theory is a crazy idea, a way of thinking about anything from fluid dynamics to economics or schizophrenia that goes against the grain of what these fields used to take for granted. The beauty of science is not to accumulate facts, but to see things differently and to say, what if we turned everything upside down. You don’t need to go far to make a discovery; you just need to realize that you’ve been wrong in your most fundamental assumptions.

But part of the allure of the book is that it is not only about the theory but also about its discovery. Gleick’s heroes – the godfathers of chaos – are often oddballs, loners, silent men in corduroy jackets who smoke too much and own extensive record collections. Some of them form tight cliques, sharing inside jokes and spending long nights together in the lab. Many are first reviled and later redeemed by the scientific community. All of them are people I desperately want to meet, and even more desperately want to be. It is depressing how even the most intellectual and Platonic ambition becomes just another signifier of personal identity. You think about how beautiful it would be to discover a completely new way of seeing the world, and then you think that you definitely need a corduroy jacket for the occasion.
