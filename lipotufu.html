Book miniatures: Cooper (ed.), Li Po and Tu Fu

My New Year’s resolution this year was to impose a diversity requirement on my reading list: every fourth book I read must be by a non-White or non-Western author. Here’s something that came out of that.

This is an anthology of the two most famous poets of the T’ang era, and perhaps the two most famous Chinese poets of all time. It comes with a comprehensive yet reader-friendly introduction that places Li Po and Tu Fu in their historical, literary and philosophical context, a primer on Chinese rhyme and metre, and a commentary on each poem that clarifies all the numerous quotations, allusions and obscure references. The selection of poems offers variety without being overwhelming. In short, Cooper really tried. And yet, I had a hard time connecting to this. I like to think of myself as multicultural and globalized and as someone who already has a fairly diverse reading list, but reading 8th-century poetry from a culture with which I have virtually no personal experience served as a healthy reminder that I still inhabit a very particular corner of the universe. It’s not that Li Po’s and Tu Fu’s work is in any way shocking or outrageous or even strictly speaking weird. It’s rather that I found it somewhat boring and mildly baffling, like, so what.

The following is, according to Cooper, the best known of all Chinese poems. (He adds: The reader will probably succeed in getting a friendly Chinese waiter to say it, in his own dialect. The edition is from 1973. It was a different time.)

Quiet night thoughts

Before my bed

there is bright moonlight

So that it seems

like frost on the ground.

Lifting my head

I watch the bright moon,

Lowering my head

I dream that I’m home.

This is nice. But, really? The best known of all Chinese poems? Here’s another one (both of them are by Li Po, who will surely forgive me the copyright infringement):

Abandon

With wine I sit

absent to Night, till

(Fallen petals

in folds of my gown)

I stagger up

to stalk the brook’s moon:

The birds are gone

and people are few!

I actually kind of like this one, but still. I was expecting more, although I’m not sure of what.

Some of the poems in the book pose more obvious challenges. Many are, for some reason, full of place names, about sailing on this particular river past this particular mountain, etc. There are the historical allusions, the symbolism, the jokes, all dutifully explained by Cooper, but still creating a layer of opacity between the text and me, like subtitles in a film, but worse. On those poems I gave up altogether. But the ones I quoted above seem so simple but are elusive nonetheless. Is it the translation? Or is it just that I don’t know what the moon looks like in China?

But then the question is whether any of this is really the point. Robert Frost has also always left me cold (no pun intended). I’m moderately bored and mildly baffled by plenty of stuff. And here’s one I genuinely enjoyed, a late poem by Tu Fu:

Night thoughts afloat

By bent grasses

in a gentle wind

Under straight mast

I’m alone tonight,

And the stars hang

above the broad plain

But moon’s afloat

in this Great River:

Oh where’s my name

among the poets?

Official rank?

‘Retired for ill-health.’

Drifting, drifting,

what am I more than

A single gull

between sky and earth?

I’m looking for something on which to blame my lack of understanding and connection, and I want to blame it on the Chinese-ness, because that would make sense. The Chinese-ness is a barrier to be overcome; that’s why I’m here in the first place. This is my own personal DEI training. I expected to get something out of this, but that expectation is just standing in my way. Instead, I’d rather just see this book as a collection of random texts, by two very random guys who lived a very long time ago in a very far away land, and some of those texts I actually kind of liked. Maybe I can get a friendly Chinese waiter to say them for me.
