Book miniatures: Damasio, Descartes' Error

I’d like to preface this post by stating that I don’t think that any of this is Descartes’ fault.

Damasio is a neurologist/-scientist, and his book Descartes’ Error from the 1990s was a turning point in how neuroscientists/-logists/psycho-/the world at large think about emotions. In the old days, emotions were considered the antipode of reason, sentimental baggage that we carry around as part of our animal heritage and that continues to impede the full exercise of rationality in its full glory. The idea that Damasio advances in his book is that on the contrary, emotions assist reason; that reason cannot function properly without emotions. His key evidence comes from patients with a particular kind of brain damage who are largely devoid of emotions as a result. Crucially, these patients also turn out to be terrible at making decisions. At work, they fail to prioritize more important tasks over minor details; they make poor financial choices; and in one episode that Damasio recounts, a patient ponders for half an hour at which day of the week to make his next neurology appointment – he is unable to come to a decision. To account for these findings, Damasio proposes what he calls the somatic marker hypothesis. According to this idea, emotions support decision-making by associating each of the possible options with a ‘gut feeling’, which allows people to sort through them more quickly and efficiently. If you are considering buying a pet, you don’t waste a huge amount of time contemplating whether or not to get an alligator. It just doesn’t feel like a good idea.

The somatic marker hypothesis is, from what the internet tells me, still contested. But the larger point that Descartes’ Error makes is that emotions are primarily physical states, and that the whole psycho-neuro crowd and the world at large should abandon Cartesian dualism and instead embrace the fact that humans are embodied beings whose emotions and feelings and sensory experiences are just as important for understanding the nature of the mind as reason writ large. The mind is not just a software running on the hardware that is the body; the mind is the body and the body is the mind. This doesn’t sound groundbreaking, which is why I’ve used the phrase ‘turning point’ in the previous paragraph. We’ve grown accustomed to this way of thinking, and if you like it, you have Damasio to thank for it.

I’ve been exposed to a fair amount of neuroscience content recently and I’m starting to get an understanding of some basic ideas that goes beyond verbatim repetition of what I read somewhere and becomes an actual intuition I can operate with. But I still feel I ultimately don't understand words like ‘experience,’ ‘emotions’, ‘reason,’ ‘sensory state,’ etc., and I found this book surprisingly difficult to read given that it is written for a lay audience and that I consider myself, with characteristic modesty, to be an eminently qualified member of this group. Reading about neuroscience or psychology always feels like reading about alchemy or astrology, in that the fundamental concepts on which the field rests seem so shaky. This is not to deny that very smart people have invested a huge amount of time and effort into defining and operationalizing these terms as carefully as possible. But to me as an outsider, these are just words that hang in the air.
