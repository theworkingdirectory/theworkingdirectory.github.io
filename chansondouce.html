Book miniatures: Slimani, Chanson douce

A short novel based on a true event, Chanson douce by the Franco-Moroccan writer Leïla Slimani tells the story of a nanny working for an upper-middle-class family in Paris who eventually murders the two children in her care. It is not a crime story. Somewhat similar to Crónica de una muerte anunciada, the novel begins with the murder and then works backwards to understand how such an act of primal violence could have happened in the middle of the world.

I don’t know if we do understand in the end. We learn a lot about Louise, the nanny: about her poverty and debts, her loveless and abusive marriage with her now deceased husband, her estranged daughter, her increasing dependence on the family she works for and her fear of losing her employment, her descent into what it would be a euphemism to call mental health problems. Shortly before the murder, it is as if all life has drowned from her life. But still, there was nothing that necessitated it; it could have not happened. In the last chapter, the police officer in charge of the murder case prepares herself for a reenactment of the crime in which she herself will play Louise, in the hope of finding a clue, getting a sense of why. The novel ends before the reenactment; if there is a clue, we do not find out.

In Hand an sich legen, a book-length essay about suicide, Jean Améry wrote that suicide can never be explained from the perspective of the living, from what he calls the rationality of life. Any kind of life is better than death, a non-suicidal person might argue, and so all suicide is irrational. But life is not better than death, because death is not anything, it is nothing. Comparing anything to death is like dividing by zero. The moment you contemplate choosing death you introduce a zero into the equation, and suddenly rationality breaks down. I am not suicidal and I have never been, but I think I see what Améry is saying and I wonder if it can tell us something about Chanson douce as well. The murder of the two children makes no sense from the perspective of the living, but Louise is past this point, and so Slimani is right in ultimately refusing to make sense of it.
