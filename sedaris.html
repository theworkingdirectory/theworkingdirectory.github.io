Book miniatures: Sedaris, Me Talk Pretty One Day

I know and like Sedaris as a regular contributor to This American Life and came across this collection of (semi-)autobiographical essays in a Little Free Library. Since my dissertation deadline is only three weeks away, I figured it would be nice to read a book that is less than a hundred years old, less than a thousand pages long, written in a language I can easily understand, and not about an obscure scientific theory that I barely understand. (Posts coming up.) It was indeed nice, it was fun, and I don’t really have anything else to say about the book as such.

In one essay, Sedaris discusses his favourite daydreams. These usually involve extraordinary athletic feats, scientific breakthroughs, erotic conquests, etc., all of which the daydream version of Sedaris is of course very nonchalant about. I found this essay very interesting because I’m a prolific daydreamer myself. The main reason why the allure of social media has always largely escaped me is that it seems such a poor substitute for simply imagining what you want to see. Sedaris’s daydreams differ somewhat from mine, but what was most striking was that he seemed perfectly indifferent towards obvious logical flaws and inconsistencies in his narratives. I find this completely unacceptable. The world of my imagination must always be a perfectly possible extension of the real world. In one of my favourite daydreams, I am part of a team of linguists that has been assembled by NASA to decipher mysterious signals intercepted in space. We realize that these signals are indeed a sign of extraterrestrial life, and at the climax of the dream, I announce to the world at a press conference that humanity has at last made contact with aliens. I always give a great speech. I readily admit that this is not the most likely future awaiting me, but I will insist that it is not impossible. Sometimes I discover small logic holes in my daydreams and spend enormous amounts of energy trying to fix them. I just find it depressing to have dreams that I know can’t become true.
