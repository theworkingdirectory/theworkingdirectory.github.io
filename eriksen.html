Book miniatures: Eriksen, Small Places, Large Issues: An Introduction to Social and Cultural Anthropology

I had a brief anthropology phase when I was in my second year of college. It largely had to do with Lévi-Strauss and my general obsession with structuralism at the time and didn’t go much further than that, but I did end up working through one or two general-purpose introductions to the field, which is the genre that Eriksen’s Small Places, Large Issues belongs to. And so reading the latter was a genuinely pleasant experience but didn’t open up a whole lot of intellectual doors for me.

Which is maybe why it was so pleasant to read. There is something deeply soothing about the experience of reading a comprehensive overview over a very complex field of knowledge and coming away from it with the feeling of, oh yeah, I knew that. It is so satisfying. It’s as if this entire field is mastered and under control and just done. All of this links back to my recent post about Bloom (which equally managed to hardly talk about the book it was supposedly about), in which I spent an inordinate amount of time just articulating my frustration at never having gotten literary studies under control. One of my more peculiar habits is making lists about various aspects of my life (or my life in general), and that includes making lists about the things I feel knowledgeable about (Lévi-Strauss yes, Shakespeare no). This is not really vanity. It’s just that I feel the need to know very precisely which parts of the world are under control and which ones are not. I often wish the world was simple, with a finite number of facts and much fewer books. I don’t indulge in this, and I genuinely try to live with unresolved contingency and complexity, but I find it difficult.
