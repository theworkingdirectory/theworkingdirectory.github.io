Book miniatures: Lewis, The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe

I’m going through an unusually severe long-Covid flare-up and tried to self-medicate by spending the day on the couch and reading a children’s book. It didn’t help.

For some reason, I never read The Chronicles of Narnia as a child, even though I was obsessed with Tolkien at the time and would surely have enjoyed Lewis as well. Now, it seems too late. I still enjoy a good story, and a good story it is; something that deserves to be called a story in the true sense rather than a narrative or a plot. But having read Lewis’s Mere Christianity only recently, I found the whiff of conservative Christian ideology that permeates The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe somewhat overpowering. (For some examples, see the sections Allusions and Religious Themes in the book’s Wikipedia article, but it’s really much worse.)

There is a German saying that a clear conscience is like good digestion: you only realize how valuable it is once you lost it. Similarly, some things bring pleasure that you take for granted as a child, but then are invariably spoiled by growing older, reading too much, thinking too much and generally becoming sceptical and critical of everything, and such things include an endearing fantasy story in which the boys go fishing with Mr Beaver while the girls do the dishes with Mrs Beaver. I wish I could enjoy this, and it makes me think that maybe if I did, it would cure me of my stress-induced long-Covid episode, and maybe the fact that I don’t is the root of all of my problems. But then I think that rather, the fact that The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe is hopelessly reactionary is part of its appeal. If I were a child, it would just be another fantasy story that I might or might not find vaguely entertaining, but it would not serve as a stand-in for a prelapsarian state of innocence in which everything is delightful and wholesome and the mere idea of which miraculously restores my immune system to its normal functioning. Nostalgia is always irrational because you yearn for something that would immediately lose all its value if you ever got hold of it.
