Book miniatures: Lawrence, Lady Chatterley's Lover

It’s been a while. I’ve been busier than ever recently and haven’t had much time for reading, let alone posting. In my defence, I’m also currently slogging my way through what I believe is the longest novel I’ve ever read. Post should be coming up in a few years.

Given that as established recently in my Bloom post, I am a failed literary scholar who does not understand how art works, I will complain about this novel entirely in philosophical terms; i.e., I will object to the proposal it puts forward. It is a nice novel, and I would call it beautiful at times, but where is the point in calling anything anything. So let’s start with the proposal; I omit the plot.

The proposal is: man must be free, and this means free to love, emotionally and physically. Society is the enemy. Society keeps the individual in chains, but the individual wants to roam free, pluck dandelions and have anal sex. (It is a very explicit text.) England is being destroyed by industrialism, which covers everything in dust and coal and masses of proletarians and their industrial bosses, who have no hearts and no love and are not real men when you think about it. Or, as one of the protagonists puts it: They can’t live. They only frustrate life. Their souls are awful inside them. Death ought to be sweet to them. And I ought to be allowed to shoot them. Most likely, humanity is doomed, but if there is any hope, it lies in finding a quiet cottage far away from the coal mines, farming the land and having loads of great anal sex. But most likely, there is no hope.

I remember reading some of Lawrence’s poetry in college. What I still recall vividly: he seems to have violently objected to the idea of wearing shoes, and if he is especially excited about an idea, he will simply repeat a verse.

I wonder if I have made my point. Living in a post-Foucault age, can I just say that this book hasn’t aged well? To the extent that it is a plea for no-fault divorce and general tolerance surrounding matters of intercourse, I’m all for it, but its proto-hippie back-to-nature worldview is naive to the point of being infuriating.

The interesting question is why I am so upset by all of this, and on some level much more so than by some piece of rabid right-wing propaganda. My theory is that it is not so much about what it says, but what it says about me. Seen from the right, I look like a wimp, overly concerned about the rights and interests of people outside my in-group, not sufficiently proud of my nation, victim of post-marxist gender ideologies, etc. I don’t really mind any of this at the end of the day. But seen from Lawrence’s point of view, I look like a heartless industrialist whose soul is covered in dust, nothing more than a machine feeding on facts and numbers and coal. And this I do mind.
