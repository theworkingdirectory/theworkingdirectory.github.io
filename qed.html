Book miniatures: Feynman, QED: The Strange Theory of Light and Matter

The title of the book is apparently the official abbreviation for ‘quantum electrodynamics,’ and part of me wants to know if this is a pun, but I’m trying not to waste time and resources googling random facts anymore. So this is a book about quantum electrodynamics. Feynman is famous for being a brilliant science writer who manages to break down even the most difficult theories in a way that his audience can understand without ever compromising on accuracy. QED (the book) not only gives a high-level big-picture introduction to the concept of quantum electrodynamics, but actually teaches the math behind it. This sounds more technical than it is. The math involves manipulating a set of diagrams in the shape of arrows, which become longer or shorter and point in different directions depending on the behaviour of light and matter. It is perfectly delightful.

I am no Feynman though and can’t possibly be expected to explain his arrows and I won’t try. (I also have eight hours of dissertation writing, a job interview and capeoira behind me today and can’t really be expected to do anything sensible. It’s 21:26.) So I will do what Feynman refuses to do, which is to just try and get the gist across, without math, arrows and accuracy.

QED is a theory that explains electromagnetic forces as the exchange of photons between electrons. It makes very precise and accurate predictions in a wide range of areas, making QED a very successful theory, but all of this is boring verbiage. What is actually interesting about the theory is that the behavior of particles is analyzed not as deterministic, but in probabilistic terms. A particle is not either here or there, but its position in space is described by a probability distribution. This does not mean that a particle is in reality in a specific position and that the probability distribution merely reflects our knowledge as observers, but the particle is truly spread out over the world as a probability field. (Think Schrödinger’s cat.) At one point, Feynman actually manages to explain the uncertainty principle using an example of a photon going through a hole and reflecting off a mirror (there are a lot of mirrors in this book), which I understood for about ten seconds until this understanding was drowned in arrows. Feynman is brilliant, and he tries very hard to simplify things, but I am chronically tired these days.

I got obsessed with quantum mechanics (in a high-level, big-picture, non-accurate way) a couple of years ago because of the many-worlds interpretation developed by Hugh Everett. The standard version of quantum mechanics holds that a particle is both here and there, the cat is both dead and alive, until someone checks. Once someone actually observes the outcome, opens the box, finds the cat being either dead or alive, it is either dead or alive. The particle is either here or there; the wave function collapses. This is very odd and, for all I know, doesn’t make a whole lot of sense to anyone. Under the many-worlds interpretation, on the other hand, what happens when you open the box to look at the cat is actually that you yourself, the observer, become part of the probability distribution: there is a certain probability that you’re looking at a dead cat and another that you’re looking at a perfectly healthy one. The cat is still both dead and alive, and you are both looking at a dead cat and a living one. You split into two; you exist simultaneously in two possible worlds, both of which are equally real. The disturbing consequence of this is that anything that can happen to you happens to some version of you. There is a possible world in which you are run over by a car tomorrow; therefore, some version of you will be run over by a car tomorrow. There is probably a possible world in which you are being run over by a car right now, and it might not feel like the present you and the run-over-by-a-car you are the same person, but you used to be until you split up. You are constantly dying the most gruesome deaths.
