Book miniatures: Orwell, A Collection of Essays

Orwell was the prototypical good guy, and I’m not being ironic. He just seems to have always been on the right side of history, a committed anti-fascist and socialist who nonetheless relentlessly called out the horrors of Stalinism and the various failings of the left in general. His politics permeates his writings, not only his novels but also his essays on other novelists – what does reading Dickens tell us about the working class? Reading Kipling about racism? Adorno would hate it, but Orwell’s writing is never tendentious but always perceptive, lucid, insightful.

There is an expression among US liberals that I come across sometimes, which is that ‘someone has good politics.’ Suppose you discuss a nonfiction book, and you might say that the author generally has very good politics, even though you disagree with them on some minor points. If someone has good politics, it means that they are one of us. In the battle of good versus evil, they’re on the side of good. The expression always struck me as odd, and thinking about Orwell makes me see why. Good politics is not something that you have, but something that you do. This is not a vacuous matter of auxiliary choice. What makes Orwell the prototypical non-ironic good guy is not that he happened to hold the right beliefs, but that he persistently examined his beliefs, being always willing to overturn them. ‘Having good politics’ is a comfortable state to be in, but doing good politics in the Orwellian sense is deeply uncomfortable because it means that you are always ready to acknowledge that you have been fundamentally wrong about something important until this very point in your life.
