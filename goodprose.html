Book miniatures: Kidder and Todd, Good Prose

I was about to start this post with the sentence ‘A comprehensive introduction to the principles of nonfiction writing, Good Prose by Tracy Kidder and Richard Todd is…,’ but the authors sternly advise against danglers, a.k.a. sentence-initial appositions, and I defer to their judgement. Kidder and Todd (a nonfiction author and his editor) advise against plenty of things that I am guilty of, and their book makes me wish I myself had an editor to watch over my shoulder and keep me in check.

I struggle significantly more with the writing I do for this blog than with the writing I do for my work. I can produce genuinely good academic prose (I believe, and I have been told), and I can do so at a remarkable speed, but the style of my blog posts tends to make me cringe. The most obvious reason is that academic writing is a genre in which I have had more practice, but another is arguably that when I write about research, I actually have something to say. I am most partial to those blog posts in which I have some sort of point, however trivial, that I want to get across, such as the ones on Bloom, Hoff or Roberts. I also tend to write my blog posts with little regard for clarity. I suspect that many of the book miniatures leave it perfectly unclear what sort of book I am even talking about. Since I hardly have an audience, I just cannot be bothered to make everything explicit, but it hurts the style. I also cannot be bothered to edit and polish these posts much anymore, which is a shame, because working on my writing was part of the motivation behind this whole exercise in the first place. It does not help that when I find time for my blog, I am usually tired.

For the future (I was tempted to write ‘Moving forward…,’ but this phrase is equally something that Kidder and Todd advise against), I set myself the following rules. Have something to say. Write less. Write as simply as possible. Don’t meander, don’t dangle, don’t enumerate. Don’t pseudo-cleft unless you have a good reason for it. Edit several times. Try harder. Be better. It’s as simple as that.
