Book miniatures: Rutherford, A Brief History of Everyone Who Ever Lived

This is one of those books whose main message is that everything is more complicated than you thought it is, but which manages to deliver this message in an unusually entertaining way. It’s a book about genetics, and what we can and (more importantly) cannot learn from DNA about who we are as a species and where we come from. On the one hand, genetics can tell us a great deal about human history, such as how often homines sapientes copulated with Neanderthals (a lot) and where the fleas that carried the Black Death to Western Europe originated (I’m not a racist, but it was China). On the other hand, genetics does not provide a scientific basis for races (nor for the Celts as an ethnic group, which somehow very much surprised me), the effect of most genes is probabilistic and complicated as heck, and we understand far too little about the effect of genes on behavior to let genetic evidence play a part in our legal system. And no, humans will not evolve the ability to fly.

It's fascinating how enjoyable a good debunking can be. I suspect what makes it so satisfying is the combination of being exposed to new information, feeling that the world has become much easier to understand, and a little schadenfreude. I’ve come up with the theory that the best educational system would be one that is centered on debunking. Rather than teaching children that Belgrade is the capital of Serbia, for example, you would teach them that many people think it is Zagreb, but that this has now been thoroughly refuted. I believe this would greatly improve learning outcomes. We may not care as much as we pretend to about broadening our knowledge of the world, but we certainly love being right when others are wrong.
