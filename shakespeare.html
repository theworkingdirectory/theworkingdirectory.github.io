Book miniatures: Bloom, Shakespeare: The Invention of the Human

It is so refreshing to not try to be a good writer.

I won’t even pretend that any of this is really about the book. But just to get the basic facts out: the book is a collection of essays about Shakespeare’s plays, it’s way too long, and I hardly read a fraction of it. Bloom was a big-name literary critic at Yale, the kind of old-fashioned Western-canon-centric guy who believes Shakespeare was the greatest writer ever and who wants everyone to shut up about post-Marxist deconstructionist readings of Henry IV or gender-queer perspectives on Twelfth Night and instead go back to the text and nothing but the text so help him God. It was the good old days. I read this book because once upon a time I was a college student majoring in CompLit for reasons that aren’t fully transparent to me anymore and I’m still stuck with the lingering sense that there is something I haven’t figured out for myself yet, which is essentially how I feel about literary criticism in general, or even about the humanities in generalissimo. Whether any of it is worth my time, or anyone’s time, and if it is, why I’m not spending any time on it and mastering it like I should master everything that is worth my time and at least theoretically within my reach. So these are important matters. Post-Bloom, there are a couple of thoughts I have, but none of which really constitute an answer to any of these questions.

Point one: bardolatry. By a rough estimate, about 15–20% of page space in this book is devoted to how great a writer Shakespeare is. At the very least, this is a huge waste of paper, not to speak of the reader’s time. From an information-theoretic point of view, Bloom is redundant to a degree that should be illegal. But of course the book is not, or at least not exclusively, in the business of information exchange. It is in the business of worship, of bardolatry. I cannot emphasize enough how deeply weird this is for me. As already established in previous posts, I’m a degenerate liberal who thinks people can essentially do whatever they like as long as they don’t exactly torture puppies, so I’m baffled by the fact that people like Bloom make it their life’s calling to convince other people that Shakespeare is a great writer. As in, who cares. I’m more than happy for people to appreciate Shakespeare, but I don’t understand (anymore?) why we’re having a public discourse around this. To some extent I can understand why we’re having a discourse around how literature works, about poetics, about rhetorics, literary history, etc., but why anyone’s value judgments on literature should be anyone else’s concern just makes no sense to me. In general, I have no interest in convincing anyone that one book or piece of music or artwork or whatever is better than another, unless the person in question is dear to my heart and I’d like to introduce them to something that they might enjoy, but this is not a matter of debating truths about the world.

There are two ways in which I need to backtrack here. First, perhaps the reason why Bloom tries to convince everyone that Shakespeare is a great writer is that everyone is dear to his heart and he would like to introduce everyone to something they might enjoy. Maybe his bardolatric zeal is not about convincing people of a truth for truth’s sake, but to show them a path towards redemption. (I’m getting tired of my theological metaphors myself at this point. I’ll stop it now.) Whether or not Shakespeare was great is as important as whether or not seed oils are healthy; the people simply must now. Second, I sense a contradiction in claiming (as I did just now) that how literature works is a valid matter of public discourse, but whether some piece of literature is any good is not. The contradiction here is that you arguably can’t establish standards of how-ness without having a standard of goodness. That is, it is impossible to understand how literature works without having some notion of what it would mean for literature to succeed in what it is trying to do.

Perhaps the main reason I ended up feeling iffy about CompLit about two months into my undergrad is that it is a field in which it is difficult, if not impossible, to establish any standards for what makes for good research. There is plenty of bad research in any field, but a bad linguistics paper you can debunk. A bad paper on Shakespeare you can at best ignore. It’s a deeply unsatisfying situation. But at the same time, if there is anything you get out of a CompLit degree – and to be clear, there are many things –, it is to learn to live with unresolved complexity, with the fact that there are way too many papers on Shakespeare out there and maybe some of them are good, whatever that would mean in the first place, and you will never read most of them, and maybe it would change your life if you read Richard III, or maybe not, and maybe you should have stayed in CompLit, and none of this you will ever know for certain.
